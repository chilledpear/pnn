China Brings New Law to prevent government employees from having Intercourse with American Nationals

Published April 23, 2025, 3:03 p.m. ET

By Liv McMahon & Tom Gerken

Share Save

In a move that has sparked global attention and internal confusion, Chinese President Xi Jinping has signed into law a sweeping policy that prohibits all Chinese government employees from engaging in any form of romantic or sexual relationship with American nationals. The law, titled The National Intimacy Security Act (NISA), was passed quietly through the Standing Committee last week and unveiled to the public via a five-line announcement on the Ministry of State Security’s WeChat page. The law reportedly aims to “safeguard the ideological integrity and emotional sovereignty of the People’s Republic of China.”

According to a leaked internal memo, NISA applies to all individuals employed in civil service, military, or state-owned enterprises. Clause 3.1 of the regulation states, “Interpersonal relations of an intimate nature, including but not limited to dating, cohabitation, or carnal exchange with citizens of the United States of America, are strictly forbidden. Violation is to be met with immediate dismissal and ideological re-education.” High-level sources confirm that the law was influenced by a growing belief in Chinese leadership that U.S. citizens are being used as “emotional espionage agents” — a tactic referred to internally as “Operation Heartstrings.” A senior advisor, speaking under anonymity, claimed, “They don’t just want our secrets. They want our souls. And sometimes... our dim sum recipes.”

Within hours of the announcement, the Ministry of Public Security launched the Sentiment Surveillance Division—a unit dedicated to monitoring “emotional irregularities” among employees. AI algorithms are reportedly being used to detect affectionate emojis, nighttime video calls to U.S. numbers, and Spotify playlists featuring more than three Taylor Swift songs in a row. The new law has caused anxiety among employees, especially those already in long-distance relationships with Americans. One government IT worker in Guangzhou lamented, “My girlfriend just sent me a care package. Now I’m scared it’s evidence.”

The U.S. State Department called the move “unprecedented and deeply personal,” adding, “We believe love knows no borders, but apparently Chinese policy does.” Meanwhile, Twitter users around the globe have dubbed the policy #LoveLockdown, with memes comparing it to dystopian romance thrillers and Cold War propaganda posters.

President Xi is expected to deliver a public statement next week during the opening of the new “National Morality and Loyalty Pavilion” in Hunan Province. Rumors suggest he may extend the ban to “certain Australians and a handful of Canadians, just to be safe.” As China tightens its grip on national loyalty and intimacy alike, analysts warn that the global dating scene may be the next unexpected casualty of geopolitics.
